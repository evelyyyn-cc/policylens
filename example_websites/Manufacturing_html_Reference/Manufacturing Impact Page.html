Policy Impact on Manufacturing

Understanding how diesel subsidy policy affects Malaysia's manufacturing sector

Overall Trends Sector Impact Export vs. Domestic

What is Industrial Production Index (IPI)?

The Industrial Production Index (IPI) measures the real output of the three main industrial sectors in Malaysia: mining, manufacturing, and electricity. It serves as a key indicator of industrial activity and economic performance.

For this analysis, we focus on how Malaysia's diesel subsidy policy affects the manufacturing sector, which contributes approximately 23% to the country's GDP and employs over 2.2 million workers.

Understanding Diesel's Role in Manufacturing

Diesel fuel plays a crucial role in manufacturing through:

  • Powering generators for electricity in factories
  • Fueling transportation of raw materials and finished products
  • Operating heavy machinery and equipment
  • Serving as a feedstock for certain chemical manufacturing processes

Changes in diesel subsidy policies directly impact manufacturing production costs, affecting competitiveness and output.

Overall Manufacturing Performance Trend

This chart shows the manufacturing index trends before and after key diesel subsidy policy changes.

The chart below shows Malaysia's manufacturing performance from 2023 to early 2025, highlighting the periods before and after major diesel subsidy policy implementations. The absolute index values (2015=100) illustrate the sector's overall performance trajectory.

Key Observations:

  • Initial Impact Period (Jun-Aug 2023): Following the first phase of subsidy rationalization, manufacturing production experienced a 2.1% decline as industries adjusted to higher fuel costs.
  • Adaptation Period (Sep-Dec 2023): Manufacturing activity stabilized as businesses implemented cost control measures and passed some costs to consumers.
  • Second Policy Adjustment (Jan 2024): The targeted subsidy implementation caused temporary volatility but less severe than the initial impact.
  • Recent Trends (Q4 2024-Q1 2025): Manufacturing has shown resilience with growth resuming despite higher fuel costs, suggesting successful adaptation to the new policy environment.
Source: Department of Statistics Malaysia, Industrial Production Index (IPI) Data, Feb 2025

Why These Sectors Are More Affected

The sectors most impacted by diesel subsidy policy changes share common characteristics:

  • Energy Intensity: Basic metals and chemical manufacturing require substantial energy inputs
  • Transport Dependencies: Food products rely heavily on transportation networks
  • Petroleum-Based Inputs: Chemicals and rubber/plastics use petroleum derivatives as raw materials
  • Limited Short-term Alternatives: These sectors have fewer immediate options to adapt their energy sources

Export vs. Domestic-Oriented Manufacturing

This comparison shows how export and domestic-oriented sectors respond differently to diesel subsidy changes.

Malaysia's manufacturing sector can be broadly divided into export-oriented industries (which produce mainly for international markets) and domestic-oriented industries (which serve the local market). Each responds differently to diesel subsidy policy changes.

Export-Oriented Manufacturing

Export-oriented sectors showed greater volatility following diesel subsidy changes:

  • Average MoM growth variation: ±1.7%
  • Most affected: Electronics, electrical equipment, rubber products
  • Recovery period: 4-5 months to return to pre-policy trend
  • Higher sensitivity due to international competitiveness constraints

Domestic-Oriented Manufacturing

Domestic-oriented sectors showed more stability but sustained impact:

  • Average MoM growth variation: ±0.9%
  • Most affected: Food products, construction materials, furniture
  • Recovery period: 2-3 months but with lower final growth rates
  • Ability to pass costs to domestic consumers provided some buffer

Economic Implications

The different responses between export and domestic-oriented manufacturing have important implications:

  • International Competitiveness: Export sectors face greater challenges as they cannot easily pass increased costs to international buyers in competitive markets
  • Consumer Prices: Domestic manufacturing sectors can partially pass costs to local consumers, contributing to inflationary pressure
  • Employment Effects: Export sectors typically employ more workers, so their volatility can have larger employment impacts
  • Policy Considerations: Targeted support mechanisms may be needed for export sectors to maintain international competitiveness
Source: Department of Statistics Malaysia, IPI for Export-Oriented Divisions and IPI for Domestic-Oriented Divisions Data, Feb 2025

Key Takeaways

Sectoral Differences

Energy-intensive and transport-dependent sectors like basic metals and chemicals experience the most significant impact from diesel subsidy changes, while less energy-intensive sectors show greater resilience.

Adaptation Period

Manufacturing sectors typically require 3-5 months to adapt to new fuel price levels, with initial negative impacts followed by gradual recovery as businesses implement cost-saving measures and pricing adjustments.

Export Vulnerability

Export-oriented manufacturing shows greater sensitivity to subsidy changes due to international competitive pressures limiting their ability to pass increased costs to customers, potentially affecting Malaysia's trade balance.

Consumer Impact

Higher production costs in domestic-oriented manufacturing sectors eventually translate to increased consumer prices for everyday goods, creating a secondary effect of the diesel subsidy policy on household expenses.
