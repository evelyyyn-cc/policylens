Malaysia's Diesel Subsidy Removal

Understanding the Impact on Consumer Price Index (CPI)

Introduction

On June 1, 2024, the Malaysian government implemented a significant policy change by removing diesel subsidies that had previously cost the nation RM14.3 billion in 2023. This decision marked a shift from the blanket subsidy approach to a targeted system that aims to provide support only to specific sectors and eligible individuals.

This analysis examines how the removal of diesel subsidies has impacted Malaysia's Consumer Price Index (CPI), with detailed breakdowns by item categories, urban versus rural areas, and individual states. Our goal is to provide clear, accessible information about this policy change and its economic implications for young Malaysians.

Key Points

  • Diesel prices in Peninsular Malaysia increased by 56% (from RM2.15 to RM3.35 per liter)
  • The government estimates annual savings of RM4 billion from this policy
  • Targeted assistance programs were implemented to help vulnerable groups
  • Prices in Sabah and Sarawak remain at RM2.15 per liter

Policy Context & Timeline

Why Remove Diesel Subsidies?

The Malaysian government identified several reasons for implementing this policy:

  • To reduce fiscal burden (diesel subsidies reached RM14.3 billion in 2023, up from RM1.4 billion in 2019)
  • To stem leakages from diesel smuggling across borders
  • To redirect funds to more targeted social welfare programs
  • To align with longer-term sustainability goals

Impact on Consumer Price Index (CPI)

What is CPI?

The Consumer Price Index (CPI) measures the cost of purchasing a constant, representative 'basket' of goods and services. It's the primary tool for measuring inflation in Malaysia. Changes in CPI directly reflect how prices of everyday items are changing for the average Malaysian consumer.

Key Finding

The diesel subsidy rationalization implemented on June 10, 2024, accounted for just 0.2% of the total Consumer Price Index (CPI) weight, resulting in a lower-than-expected inflation rate of 2% in June 2024.

Urban vs Rural Impact

The impact of diesel subsidy removal shows distinctive patterns between urban and rural areas. This reflects the different consumption patterns, transportation needs, and economic structures in these areas.

Urban Areas

Urban residents generally experienced a lower direct impact from diesel price increases, partly due to more public transportation options and shorter commute distances. However, urban areas saw higher indirect impacts through services and goods that depend on transportation.

Key Urban Impacts

  • Food delivery and e-commerce costs increased
  • Restaurant prices saw modest increases
  • Public transportation largely shielded from impacts

Rural Areas

Rural communities felt a stronger direct impact from the diesel price increase due to greater dependence on private transportation, longer commute distances, and higher reliance on diesel-powered agricultural equipment.

Key Rural Impacts

  • Agricultural production costs increased
  • Higher transportation expenses for daily commute
  • Increased costs for goods due to higher logistics expenses
  • BUDI Agri-Komoditi program partially offset impacts

State-by-State Impact Analysis

The impact of diesel subsidy removal varies significantly across Malaysia's states, influenced by geographic location, economic structure, and local policies.

Key Regional Difference

Sabah and Sarawak were exempted from the diesel subsidy removal, with prices remaining at RM2.15 per liter. This has created a significant regional disparity in inflation rates post-implementation.

Highest Impact States

The East Coast states (Kelantan, Terengganu) experienced the highest impact due to:

  • Higher dependence on road transportation
  • Longer distances to major economic centers
  • Greater proportion of primary sector activities (agriculture, fishing)

Lowest Impact States

East Malaysia (Sabah, Sarawak) experienced the lowest impact due to:

  • Exemption from the diesel subsidy removal
  • Maintained diesel prices at RM2.15 per liter
  • Government policy acknowledging the unique economic challenges

Broader Economic Implications

Fiscal Impact

The diesel subsidy rationalization is projected to save the government approximately RM4 billion annually, equivalent to 0.2% of Malaysia's GDP. These savings contribute to the government's broader fiscal consolidation efforts aimed at reducing the budget deficit.

Diesel Subsidy Spending

2019: RM1.4 billion

2023: RM14.3 billion

2024 (projected): RM10.3 billion

Inflation Outlook

Despite the diesel subsidy removal, the government maintained its 2024 inflation projection at 2-3.5%. The Bank Negara Malaysia's positive real interest rate of 1% indicates that the current monetary policy remains quite restrictive, supporting economic stability.

Key Inflation Indicators

June 2024 CPI: 2.0% (year-on-year)

Transport inflation: 1.2% (up from 0.9% in May)

Food inflation: 0.9% (up from 0.5% in March)

Winners & Losers

Winners

  • Government fiscal position: Reduced subsidy burden
  • Public sector financing: More funds for targeted social programs
  • Long-term economic sustainability
  • Fuel retailers: Potentially higher margins

Losers

  • Rural communities: Higher transportation costs
  • Small-scale farmers: Increased production costs
  • Logistics companies not covered by exemptions
  • Middle-income households: Higher indirect costs

Long-Term Projections

The longer-term economic impact of diesel subsidy removal is expected to be positive:

  • More efficient allocation of resources
  • Reduced fiscal vulnerability to oil price fluctuations
  • Better targeted support for vulnerable groups
  • Reduced incentives for smuggling and leakages
  • Gradual transition toward more sustainable energy consumption

Economists expect the initial inflation impact to stabilize by Q4 2024, with long-term benefits outweighing short-term adjustments.

Targeted Assistance Programs

To mitigate the impact of diesel subsidy removal, the government implemented several targeted assistance programs designed to help specific sectors and vulnerable groups:

BUDI Individu

RM200 monthly cash assistance for Malaysian individuals owning diesel vehicles to help offset higher fuel expenditure.

First payment: June 10, 2024

BUDI Agri-Komoditi

RM200 monthly cash assistance for small-scale farmers and commodity smallholders to mitigate impact on their incomes.

First payment: June 10, 2024

SKDS Programs

Fleet cards and subsidized rates for logistics vehicles (SKDS 2.0) and public transport (SKDS 1.0) to maintain price stability.

Implemented: June 10, 2024

Implementation Challenges

Despite these targeted programs, some implementation challenges have emerged:

  • Registration requirements for BUDI programs exclude many small-scale farmers
  • Delays in fleet card issuance for some eligible logistics companies
  • Limited awareness among eligible recipients about available assistance
  • Application process complexity for some vulnerable groups

Frequently Asked Questions

Why did the government remove diesel subsidies? +
The government removed diesel subsidies primarily to reduce fiscal burden (subsidies reached RM14.3 billion in 2023), prevent leakages from smuggling, and redirect funds to more targeted social welfare programs. This move is part of a broader strategy to improve Malaysia's fiscal position and ensure subsidies benefit those who truly need them.
How much has diesel price increased after subsidy removal? +
In Peninsular Malaysia, diesel prices increased by 56%, from RM2.15 to RM3.35 per liter. However, Sabah, Sarawak, and Labuan continue to enjoy the subsidized rate of RM2.15 per liter. Certain sectors like public transport, logistics, and fisheries have access to lower prices through targeted subsidy mechanisms.
Who qualifies for the BUDI assistance programs? +
BUDI Individu is available to Malaysian individuals who own diesel vehicles. BUDI Agri-Komoditi is for small-scale farmers and commodity smallholders. To qualify for BUDI Agri-Komoditi, farmers must be registered with relevant agencies and have an annual turnover between RM50,000 and RM300,000 from farming and commodities. Applications can be made through the BUDI MADANI portal at budimadani.gov.my.
How has the subsidy removal affected inflation? +
The impact has been relatively modest. The diesel subsidy rationalization implemented on June 10, 2024, accounted for just 0.2% of the total Consumer Price Index (CPI) weight. In June 2024, the overall inflation rate was 2.0%, with the transport category inflation increasing from 0.9% in May to 1.2% in June. The government maintains its inflation outlook at 2-3.5% for 2024.
Will RON95 petrol subsidies also be removed? +
The government has announced plans to reform RON95 subsidies as well. Budget 2025 states that RON95 subsidies will be removed for the top 15% of income earners starting in mid-2025. This indicates a continued shift from blanket subsidies to targeted approaches, but with a more gradual implementation timeline compared to diesel.
How does the PADU system relate to subsidy reforms? +
The Central Database Hub (PADU) system is a crucial component of Malaysia's targeted subsidy approach. It collects comprehensive household information to help the government identify eligible recipients for various assistance programs. PADU data helps ensure that subsidies and aid reach those who truly need them, supporting the shift from blanket subsidies to targeted approaches. The database improves the government's understanding of household economic situations across different income levels and locations.

Conclusion

The removal of diesel subsidies in Malaysia represents a significant shift in government policy toward more targeted approaches to economic support. While the immediate inflationary impact has been relatively modest, the effects vary across different sectors, regions, and demographic groups.

Key findings from our analysis include:

  • The overall inflation impact has been contained at 2.0% in June 2024
  • Rural areas experienced slightly higher impacts compared to urban centers
  • East Coast states saw the highest inflation effects, while East Malaysia was largely shielded
  • Targeted assistance programs have helped mitigate impacts for vulnerable groups
  • The government is expected to save RM4 billion annually, strengthening fiscal position

This policy change underscores a broader trend toward fiscal sustainability and more efficient allocation of government resources. While short-term adjustments may cause temporary hardships for some groups, the long-term benefits of reduced fiscal vulnerability and better-targeted assistance are expected to contribute positively to Malaysia's economic resilience.

As Malaysia continues its subsidy rationalization journey, with RON95 reforms planned for 2025, ongoing monitoring of economic impacts and continuous refinement of assistance programs will be essential to ensuring a balanced and inclusive transition.
